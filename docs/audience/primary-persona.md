These are real expressions captured from the target demographic:

> *"Three thousand dollars to stick something in my ear that everyone can see? I'm not there yet."*

> *"I hear fine in quiet rooms. It's only in restaurants and when there's background noise."*

> *"If my audiologist told me to put these on, I'd do it. I trust her."*

> *"The worst part isn't not hearing. The worst part is pretending I did hear."*

> *"My dad wore hearing aids and everyone treated him differently after that."*

> *"I don't want charity glasses or pity glasses. I want something that makes me better at being me."*

> *"I've got three pairs of glasses and none of them do everything I need."*

> *"I told the audiologist I'd think about it. That was two years ago."*

> *"Every assistive device is another step toward losing your independence."*

## Pain Points

1. **Hearing aid stigma** prevents adoption despite acknowledged need
2. **Cost barrier** — $3,200 hearing aids on a fixed income feels irresponsible
3. **Generic OTC amplifiers** make everything louder, including noise
4. **Multiple glasses pairs** create daily frustration and cognitive load
5. **Social withdrawal** from restaurants, church, golf, and family events
6. **Family tension** from repeated "What?" and avoidance behavior
7. **Pretending to hear** erodes dignity and self-image
8. **Technology anxiety** around complicated devices and apps
9. **Fear that assistive devices** signal loss of independence
10. **7–10 year gap** between hearing loss onset and first action

## The Purchase Journey

### Awareness
George doesn't search for "hearing aids." He Googles "why can't I hear in restaurants" or mentions it to his optometrist. His daughter Sarah may research on his behalf after noticing the TV volume at 40.

### Consideration
George responds to **professional recommendation**, not advertising. If his audiologist or optometrist says "I think you should try these," George listens. He does not respond to fear-based messaging, urgency tactics, or tech hype.

### Decision
- **Price must feel responsible** — $249–$349 passes the "fixed income" test
- **Form factor must be invisible** — looks like regular glasses
- **Professional endorsement required** — clinic recommendation is the trigger
- **Sarah's research helps** — she finds information, shares it with George, validates the recommendation

### Post-Purchase
George wears WHSPR Assist daily, replacing his previous non-smart glasses. Usage creates increasing dependency and switching costs. He becomes an organic referral source through golf buddies, church friends, and family networks.
