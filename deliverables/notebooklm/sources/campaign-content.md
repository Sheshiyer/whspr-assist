## Campaign Page Copy

The campaign page is the central conversion asset for the WHSPR Assist Rx crowdfunding launch. It runs 5,492 words and is written in the voice of Dr. Maren, the brand's Compassionate Companion persona. The tone is calibrated to warmth level 8 out of 10, formality level 4 out of 10, and targets a Grade 6–7 reading level — accessible, human, and never clinical.

### Above the Fold

The headline reads: **"The glasses your audiologist wishes existed ten years ago."**

The subheadline follows: **"Prescription-ready smart glasses that combine hearing enhancement, AI vision assistance, and everyday eyewear in a single frame. For under $350."**

The primary call-to-action button is: **"Back This Project — Early Bird $249"**

This opening section establishes the core value proposition in a single scroll. The headline operates on borrowed authority — the audiologist as a trusted proxy — and positions WHSPR Assist Rx not as a tech gadget but as a long-overdue solution that professionals in the field have been waiting for. The subhead compresses the three core capabilities (hearing enhancement, AI vision assistance, everyday eyewear) into one sentence, and the price anchor lands immediately: under $350.

### The Old World: Life Before WHSPR Assist

This section paints the status quo that the target audience lives in every day. It describes the experience of missing conversations at family dinners, pretending to hear jokes at restaurants, squinting at medicine bottles and restaurant menus, and the quiet shame of asking people to repeat themselves — again. The narrative centers on the emotional cost of doing nothing, which is the reality for 80 percent of the 53 million Americans who have hearing loss but use no device at all.

The copy introduces the core tension: traditional hearing aids cost $3,200 or more per pair, carry social stigma, require multiple clinic visits, and still don't help you read the small print on a prescription label. Meanwhile, regular glasses just sit on your face doing one job. The old world is one of fragmented, expensive, stigmatized solutions — or, far more often, no solution at all.

### The New World: What Changes with WHSPR Assist Rx

The new world section introduces the product as the resolution to every pain point raised in the old world. WHSPR Assist Rx is described as a single pair of prescription-ready smart glasses that enhance hearing using dual-microphone ANC/ENC directional audio, read text aloud using a 32MP AI-enhanced camera, and look like ordinary eyewear because they are built on lightweight TR thermoplastic frames weighing under 44 grams.

The price anchor is made explicit: **"Traditional hearing aids plus prescription glasses plus an AI vision device would cost you $7,000 to $11,300. WHSPR Assist Rx starts at $249 and does all three."** This comparison — $7,000–$11,300 versus $249–$349 — is the economic engine of the entire campaign.

### Feature Triads

The campaign page presents 10 feature triads, each consisting of a feature name, a functional description, and an emotional benefit. These triads are the building blocks of the product story and recur throughout ads, emails, and the video script.

The features covered include directional hearing enhancement with dual-mic ANC/ENC technology, AI-powered text reading via the 32MP camera system, prescription-ready TR thermoplastic frames in four styles, magnetic charging cradle with all-day battery, Bluetooth 5.4 hands-free calling, the Companion app with audiogram-based hearing profile calibration, Hey Cyan voice activation, the Provider Portal for audiologists and optometrists, HSA/FSA eligibility, and the four frame style options (Classic, Heritage, Refined, and Active).

### Social Proof Section

Four testimonials anchor the social proof section, each mapped to a campaign persona:

**George Mitchell, age 68** — the primary end user. George represents the 80 percent who have hearing loss but refuse traditional hearing aids due to stigma and cost. His testimonial speaks to dignity, independence, and reconnecting with his granddaughter Lily at the dinner table.

**Sarah Mitchell, age 42** — George's daughter and the caregiver persona. Sarah's testimonial captures the relief of seeing her father participate in conversation again, and the emotional weight of watching someone you love withdraw from the people around them.

**Dr. Priya Sharma** — the audiologist and professional channel persona. Dr. Priya validates WHSPR Assist Rx from a clinical perspective, emphasizing that 40 to 60 percent of patients she evaluates for hearing loss decline traditional hearing aids. WHSPR Assist Rx, she says, changes the conversation entirely.

**Marcus Williams** — a secondary persona representing the active, tech-forward older adult who wants hearing help but would never wear a traditional hearing aid.

### Risk Reversal

The risk reversal section addresses the five most common objections: "Will it work for my hearing loss?", "Can I get my actual prescription in these?", "What if I don't like them?", "Is this FDA-cleared?", and "How is this so affordable?"

The answers reference the OTC hearing aid FDA category established by the August 2022 ruling for mild-to-moderate hearing loss, the prescription-ready frame system compatible with standard single-vision and progressive lenses, the satisfaction guarantee, and the cost reduction enabled by bypassing traditional hearing aid distribution markup — resulting in a 90 to 95 percent cost reduction versus traditional devices.

### FAQ Section

The page includes 12 frequently asked questions covering topics such as prescription compatibility, battery life, hearing profile calibration, frame sizing, the Companion app, Provider Portal access, HSA/FSA eligibility, shipping timelines, the difference between WHSPR Assist Rx and traditional hearing aids, AI vision capabilities, return policy, and international availability.

### Final Offer and CTA

The final offer section restates the tier pricing, reinforces the value comparison, and closes with the CTA: **"Back WHSPR Assist Rx — Choose Your Frame Style"**

### Campaign Pricing Tiers

The campaign offers four pledge tiers:

- **Super Early Bird:** $249 — limited to 200 units. This is the lowest price WHSPR Assist Rx will ever be offered at and is the primary urgency driver across all advertising.
- **Early Bird:** $279 — limited to 300 units. Available after Super Early Bird sells out.
- **Standard:** $299 — unlimited quantity. The base campaign price.
- **Caregiver Bundle:** $499 — limited to 100 units. Includes two pairs of WHSPR Assist Rx, designed for the Sarah Mitchell persona buying for both parents.

## Campaign Video Script

The hero video is the primary audiovisual asset for the WHSPR Assist Rx crowdfunding launch. It runs 150 seconds (2 minutes and 30 seconds), shot in 16:9 at 4K resolution. The voiceover totals 382 words delivered at 2.55 words per second. The production calls for 20 B-roll shots, 3 testimonial segments, 21 on-screen text cards, 8 music cues, 7 filming locations, 5 principal cast members, and 8 background extras.

### Music and Emotional Arc

The music follows a deliberate emotional arc that mirrors the narrative structure. It begins with a solo fingerpicked acoustic guitar during the hook — intimate, slightly melancholic, evoking a quiet moment of isolation. Soft piano enters during the problem section, adding warmth without breaking the reflective tone. Warm strings join at the product reveal, marking the emotional lift — the moment the viewer feels hope. Light percussion enters during the how-it-works section, grounding the story in practical clarity. The full arrangement — guitar, piano, strings, and soft drums — builds through the feature highlights, conveying momentum and capability. The arrangement strips back to piano and guitar for the social proof testimonials, creating intimacy and trust. Piano with light percussion carries the offer section, keeping the tone warm but purposeful. The video resolves with the full arrangement tapering to a solo piano, closing the emotional circle.

### Section 1: Hook (0:00–0:10, 10 seconds)

The emotional goal is recognition — the viewer identifies with the moment before a single word is spoken. The camera opens on a close-up of George at a family dinner table. The audio is presented from George's perspective — muffled, indistinct, the laughter and conversation around him blurred into noise. Solo fingerpicked acoustic guitar plays underneath muffled dinner ambience. The viewer feels the isolation before understanding it intellectually.

### Section 2: Problem and Hero (0:10–0:30, 20 seconds)

The emotional goal shifts to empathy and identification with George's story. A documentary-style montage unfolds: George at the dinner table straining to hear, George and his wife at a noisy restaurant, a close-up of a prescription bottle with small print, George in a clinic being shown traditional hearing aids and subtly declining. The acoustic guitar continues with soft piano entering. The viewer understands this is not just George's story — it is their story, or their father's story, or their patient's story.

### Section 3: Product Reveal (0:30–0:45, 15 seconds)

The emotional goal is curiosity and surprise — these are just glasses. Dr. Priya hands a pair of WHSPR Assist Rx glasses to George across a clinical desk. George puts them on and looks in a mirror. The viewer sees his expression shift. A product beauty shot follows — the glasses isolated against a clean background, rotating slowly, looking like nothing more than a well-made pair of everyday eyewear. Piano leads with warm strings. The emotional lift lands here.

### Section 4: How It Works (0:45–1:15, 30 seconds)

The emotional goal is clarity and confidence — a straightforward three-step process. The sequence shows: Step one, George at the clinic as Dr. Priya fits the frames and calibrates his hearing profile through the Companion app. Step two, George's morning routine — he picks up the glasses from the magnetic charging cradle, puts them on like any other pair of glasses. Step three, George at the breakfast table with his wife, hearing her clearly, responding naturally. Light percussion enters, grounding the sequence in practical reality.

### Section 5: Feature Highlights (1:15–1:45, 30 seconds)

The emotional goal is to leave the viewer impressed and informed. A split-audio demonstration shows George in a noisy café — the viewer hears what George hears with enhancement versus without. The AI text reading is demonstrated: George points his glasses at a restaurant menu and hears it read aloud through the bone conduction speakers. A frame lineup shows all four styles — Classic (V03-T5), Heritage (V03-T6), Refined (V03-T8), and Active (V06-T) — alongside a lens-fitting sequence showing prescription compatibility. The full musical arrangement plays: guitar, piano, strings, and soft drums.

### Section 6: Social Proof (1:45–2:00, 15 seconds)

The emotional goal is trust and validation from three distinct perspectives. Three talking-head testimonials, each delivered directly to camera:

George Mitchell says something to the effect of regaining dignity and connection with his granddaughter. His testimonial speaks from lived experience — the person who almost did nothing and is grateful he didn't.

Dr. Priya Sharma speaks about capturing the patients who decline hearing aids — the 40 to 60 percent who walk out of her clinic every day with nothing. WHSPR Assist Rx, she says, gives her something to offer them.

Sarah Mitchell speaks about relief — seeing her father back in the conversation, participating instead of performing.

The music strips back to piano and guitar, creating intimacy.

### Section 7: Offer (2:00–2:15, 15 seconds)

The emotional goal is value clarity — the price comparison must land. A price comparison graphic appears: traditional hearing aids at $3,200 or more on one side, WHSPR Assist Rx at $249 on the other. A product flat lay shows the glasses, the charging cradle, and the carrying case. Tier pricing appears on screen: Early Bird $249, Standard $299, Complete Kit $349. Piano with light percussion carries the section.

### Section 8: Call to Action (2:15–2:30, 15 seconds)

The emotional goal is warm resolution and motivated action. The camera returns to George and his granddaughter Lily at the dinner table — but now the audio is clear, present, alive. George laughs at something Lily says. He heard it. The WHSPR Assist logo appears, followed by the tagline and the campaign URL. The full arrangement resolves to a solo piano, closing the emotional circle that began with the solo guitar.

## Cross-Document Creative Patterns

Several creative patterns recur across all campaign materials, forming the connective tissue of the WHSPR Assist Rx brand narrative:

### The Price Anchor

The comparison of $7,000–$11,300 (traditional hearing aids plus prescription glasses plus an AI vision device) versus $249–$349 (WHSPR Assist Rx) appears in the campaign page, the video script, the pre-launch ads, the live campaign ads, and the press release. It is the single most repeated data point in the creative library.

### The 80 Percent Statistic

The fact that 80 percent of Americans with hearing loss use no hearing device is the foundational market insight that justifies the product's existence. It appears in the campaign page's old world section, the founder story ad headlines, the press release lead, and the video script's problem section.

### The Audiologist as Authority

The audiologist — personified by Dr. Priya Sharma — serves as the borrowed-authority figure across all channels. The campaign page headline ("The glasses your audiologist wishes existed ten years ago"), the pre-launch ad HY-2 (same headline), the live campaign ad LCA-LN-2 ("Your audiologist has been waiting for this"), and the press release quote all leverage the audiologist's credibility.

### The Dignity Frame

Rather than positioning WHSPR Assist Rx as a medical device or a tech gadget, the creative consistently frames it as a dignity tool. George does not "get hearing help" — he hears his granddaughter's story at dinner. The emotional selling headlines (ES-1, ES-2, ES-3) and the video script's hook and resolution sections both center on what hearing loss takes away emotionally, not medically.

### The Four Personas

George Mitchell (68, end user), Sarah Mitchell (42, caregiver daughter), Dr. Priya Sharma (audiologist/professional channel), and Marcus Williams (active/tech-forward older adult) appear across the campaign page, video script, pre-launch ads, live ads, and press release. George is the primary persona in 33 of 39 pre-launch ad assets. Sarah drives the Caregiver Bundle tier and the secondary ad targeting. Dr. Priya provides professional validation. Marcus represents the aspirational user who wants capability without stigma.

### Voice Consistency

All consumer-facing copy (campaign page, ads, video script) is written in the Dr. Maren voice — the Compassionate Companion tone calibrated to warmth 8, formality 4, and Grade 6–7 reading level. The press release shifts to AP-style professional tone but maintains the same core messaging and data points. This tonal split is intentional: the press release speaks to journalists and industry professionals, while all other creative speaks directly to end users and caregivers.
