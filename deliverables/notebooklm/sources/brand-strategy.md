## Product Description

### What WHSPR Assist Is

WHSPR Assist Rx is the first assistive smart glasses platform that combines hearing enhancement, AI-powered vision assistance, and prescription-ready eyewear in a single frame. The retail price is $249 to $349. The wholesale price for clinical partners is $120 to $140. The underlying technology platform is V03/V06, built on the JL7018F chipset.

The product is classified as hearing enhancement, not a hearing aid. It targets adults with mild-to-moderate hearing difficulty — the 80 percent of Americans with hearing loss who are not currently wearing hearing aids. The average delay between recognizing hearing difficulty and seeking treatment is 8.9 years. WHSPR Assist intercepts that delay by meeting people where they already are: wearing glasses.

### The Four Models

The product line includes four distinct frame styles across two platform variants.

The lifestyle line runs on the V03 platform. The Classic model, designated V03-T5, features a square full-rim frame with a 220mAh battery. The Heritage model, designated V03-T6, offers a round frame silhouette with the same 220mAh battery. The Refined model, designated V03-T8, presents a semi-rimless design, also with a 220mAh battery. These three frames share identical internal components and differ only in aesthetics, allowing the wearer to choose a frame that matches their personal style rather than accepting a one-size-fits-all medical appliance.

The sport line runs on the V06 platform. The Active model, designated V06-T, uses a sport wrap frame with a larger 260mAh battery and 4GB of onboard storage. It is designed for outdoor activities — golf, walking, cycling — where durability, a secure fit, and extended battery life matter.

### Materials and Construction

Every WHSPR Assist frame is constructed from TR thermoplastic, the same frame material used by premium optical brands. TR offers a combination of lightweight comfort, flexibility, and durability that makes all-day wear practical. The temple arms incorporate gesture-sensitive touch tips for tap-to-activate controls. Silicone nose pads ensure a stable, comfortable fit across different face shapes. The lens system accepts standard prescription grinding from any optometrist, which means the wearer does not need a special provider or proprietary lens.

Audio is delivered through dual open-ear directional speakers built into the temple arms. Two microphones provide active noise cancellation and echo noise cancellation, isolating speech from ambient noise. Charging is magnetic — a two-pin connector that snaps into place, eliminating the fumbling with tiny batteries that ranks as the fourth most common complaint about traditional hearing aids.

### The Ten Core Features

Each feature is described here with its benefit and the proof point that supports the claim.

**Dual-Microphone ANC/ENC.** Two microphones with active noise cancellation and echo noise cancellation deliver open-ear directional audio. The benefit is direct: you hear the person talking to you, not every fork scraping a plate and every air conditioner humming. Conversations in noisy restaurants, family dinners, and church services become clear again without plugging anything into your ear. The technology is the same dual-mic ANC/ENC processing used in professional conference headsets.

**Prescription-Ready TR Frames.** Durable, lightweight TR thermoplastic frames accept prescription lenses from any optometrist. One pair does everything — distance, reading, hearing, and AI assistance. No more juggling three pairs of glasses. No more leaving the readers at home.

**1200p Dual Camera System (8MP + 32MP AI).** Two cameras — an 8-megapixel wide-angle and a 32-megapixel AI-optimized lens — are embedded in the frame. The AI reads prescription labels aloud, describes scenes, recognizes faces, and identifies objects. Comparable vision-assistive devices like OrCam MyEye cost $3,500 to $4,500 for similar camera-based AI reading. WHSPR Assist delivers the same category of functionality at roughly one-tenth the price.

**Bluetooth 5.4 Connectivity.** The newest Bluetooth standard enables low-latency streaming and hands-free calls. Phone calls are clear and natural. Music, podcasts, and audiobooks stream directly. BT 5.4 exceeds even Phonak's 5.3, the standard used by the world's leading hearing aid brand. Lower latency means voices sync with lip movement during video calls.

**Zero-Stigma Smart Glasses Form Factor.** The device looks like a regular pair of contemporary eyeglasses. No behind-ear module, no visible in-ear dome, no clip-on attachment. Research shows that 66 percent of people with hearing loss say they would wear a hearing device if it were invisible. Meanwhile, 92.4 percent of adults 65 and older already wear glasses daily. WHSPR Assist turns the most normalized accessory in America into a hearing solution.

**Magnetic Two-Pin Charging.** A magnetic charging connector snaps into place. Set the glasses down on the charging stand at night, pick them up in the morning. No tiny batteries to fumble. The same bedside routine as taking off regular glasses, except these charge while you sleep. Magnetic 2-pin charging eliminates the number-four complaint about traditional hearing aids: battery management.

**AI-Powered Scene and Text Recognition.** On-device AI processing reads text, describes scenes, identifies objects, and recognizes faces through the dual camera system. The glasses whisper what your eyes miss — a restaurant menu, a street sign, the name tag on the new nurse at the doctor's office. This category of AI vision assistance previously required a $4,500 OrCam or $3,500 Envision device. WHSPR packages it into a sub-$350 pair of glasses alongside hearing enhancement, making the 32MP AI camera comparable to the OrCam at approximately one-thirteenth the price.

**B2B Provider Portal.** Clinics order at wholesale ($120 to $140), fit with prescription lenses, and track patient engagement through a Provider Portal. Clinicians capture the 40 to 60 percent of patients who decline hearing aids. They earn $100 or more in margin per unit. They differentiate their practice with a product no competitor carries. They track patient usage data for follow-up and upsell. Independent audiology practices that are losing patients to Costco (16 percent market share) and OTC alternatives can use WHSPR Assist to create a new product category that brings patients back into the clinic.

**Three Frame Styles for Lifestyle Fit.** Classic for everyday, Heritage for a timeless look, Refined for professional settings, Active for the golf course and morning walks. Frame variety signals "lifestyle product," not "medical device." This is how premium optical brands build loyalty.

**Whitelabel and Shadow Whitelabel Capability.** Clinics and optical chains can brand WHSPR Assist under their own practice name, including device, packaging, and companion app. No hearing aid manufacturer offers true whitelabel branding. Clinic-branded devices create switching costs and patient loyalty that commodity hearing aids cannot replicate.

### Known Limitations

Honesty about limitations is central to brand credibility. WHSPR Assist has no completed speech-in-noise validation testing. Battery life is 4 to 5 hours of active use. Insurance does not cover the product. It provides enhancement only, not amplification — it is not a hearing aid, and it is not intended to replace one. The product is appropriate for mild-to-moderate hearing difficulty only. For moderate-to-severe hearing loss, clinical hearing aids remain the recommendation.

## Positioning Framework — Customer-Based Brand Equity

### Salience

WHSPR Assist occupies a new micro-category. It is a category of one. No other product on the market combines prescription vision correction, hearing enhancement, and AI-powered assistance in a single frame at any price, let alone under $1,000. The purchase trigger is the moment someone realizes they need hearing help but will not wear hearing aids.

The market opportunity is substantial. More than 53 million Americans experience hearing loss. Only 20 percent are currently treated. The average delay between recognizing hearing difficulty and seeking treatment is 8.9 years. And 92.4 percent of adults aged 65 and older already wear glasses every day. WHSPR Assist sits at the intersection of these facts: an untreated majority that already wears the form factor.

### Performance

The technical performance story centers on three pillars. First, audio: open-ear directional speakers with dual-microphone ANC/ENC and audiogram shaping deliver hearing enhancement that isolates speech from background noise. Second, vision: a dual camera system with 8MP and 32MP AI lenses captures 1200p video and powers text recognition, scene description, and face identification. Third, connectivity: Bluetooth 5.4 enables low-latency streaming and hands-free calls, exceeding the BT 5.3 standard used by Phonak.

The frames are TR thermoplastic, available in four styles, and accept standard prescription lens grinding. Magnetic charging eliminates battery management frustration. The companion app provides audiogram calibration, hearing profile tracking, and remote recalibration. The Provider Portal gives clinicians patient engagement data, ordering tools, and whitelabel configuration.

### Imagery

The brand imagery targets active adults aged 55 to 75 who value independence and dignity. The visual world of WHSPR Assist is family dinners, coffee with friends, golf courses, church services, morning routines, and garden reading. The brand energy draws from AARP Magazine, Consumer Reports, Warby Parker, and Bose — trustworthy, clean, accessible, and intelligent without being clinical.

The anti-imagery is equally specific. WHSPR Assist is not a medical device. It is not a tech gadget. It is not a luxury accessory. It is not a cheap amplifier. It never looks clinical, never looks flashy, never looks patronizing.

### Judgments

The product must pass four judgment tests in the mind of each audience.

Form: "Finally, something I would actually wear." The glasses look like glasses, not medical equipment. This is the first and most important judgment because it determines whether the product is even considered.

Sufficiency: "This works well enough." For mild-to-moderate hearing difficulty, the enhancement does not need to match a $4,000 hearing aid. It needs to make dinner conversations audible and phone calls clear.

Value: "The price is right." At $249 to $349, WHSPR Assist costs less than most hearing aid copays after insurance. No pre-authorization, no coverage denial. HSA and FSA accounts typically cover assistive technology for pre-tax purchasing.

Credibility: "My audiologist recommended it." Professional endorsement is the most powerful trust signal for the 55-to-75 demographic and the primary reason the B2B clinical channel is strategically essential.

Adoption: "He will actually wear these." The glasses form factor removes the top objection to hearing devices.

Validation: "A professional recommended it — this is not another Amazon impulse buy."

**Objections and Responses.** Every honest positioning framework names the doubts the brand will face.

First objection: "These are not real hearing aids." Correct, and that is the point. Real hearing aids cost $3,200, sit in a drawer for 60 percent of people who buy them, and make you look like a patient. WHSPR Assist is hearing enhancement built into glasses you already wear. For mild-to-moderate hearing difficulty — what 80 percent of untreated Americans have — it delivers the clarity needed at a price that respects the budget. If hearing changes over time, the audiologist can recommend the next step. These keep people in care instead of doing nothing.

Second objection: "The battery only lasts 4 to 5 hours." Fair point. Magnetic snap charging takes about an hour. For comparison, you take off regular glasses when you nap or rest your eyes. Same routine, except these charge while they rest. Most users find that hearing enhancement matters most in specific situations — dinner, church, golf, phone calls — and 4 to 5 hours covers those windows comfortably. The next-generation model targets all-day battery.

Third objection: "I can get hearing enhancement from AirPods Pro." AirPods Pro offer basic hearing enhancement through Apple's clinical-grade audiogram feature. However, AirPods block natural ambient sound, require separate charging, look conspicuous on a 65-year-old at Thanksgiving dinner, and offer zero vision assistance. WHSPR Assist is open-ear (you hear the world naturally around you), built into your glasses (nothing in your ears), includes AI vision (reads text, recognizes faces), and is prescribed by your audiologist — not configured through a phone app alone.

Fourth objection: "My patients need clinical-grade hearing aids, not consumer electronics." Absolutely — for moderate-to-severe loss, clinical hearing aids remain the recommendation. WHSPR Assist is for the 40 to 60 percent of patients who decline hearing aids and disappear from care. It keeps them in the practice, tracks their hearing trends via the Provider Portal, and creates a natural pathway to hearing aids when their loss progresses. It is the entry ramp, not the destination.

Fifth objection: "Nobody has heard of this brand." Correct. WHSPR Assist is new. But it is sold through licensed hearing and vision professionals who stake their reputation on every product they recommend. The technology inside — JL7018F audio chip, dual-mic ANC/ENC, 32MP AI camera, BT 5.4 — is built on proven components used across professional audio and imaging industries. The brand is new. The technology is not.

Sixth objection: "I already wear hearing aids. Why would I switch?" You probably should not switch. If your hearing aids are working well, keep using them. WHSPR Assist is designed for the 80 percent of Americans with hearing loss who have not adopted any solution. If you know someone who refuses hearing aids — a spouse, a parent, a friend — this might be the thing they will actually put on their face.

Seventh objection: "At $120 wholesale, the quality cannot be good." The $120 to $140 wholesale price reflects consumer electronics economics, not hearing aid economics. Traditional hearing aids carry $1,500 or more in wholesale costs because of decades of R&D amortization, proprietary chip licensing, and audiologist-exclusive distribution margins. WHSPR uses commercially available components — JL7018F chip, Sony camera sensors, standard TR thermoplastic — at volume pricing. The quality comparison should be to Ray-Ban Meta ($379 retail) or Solos AirGo ($299 retail), which use the same class of components at the same price tier, not to a $6,000 Starkey with proprietary silicon.

Eighth objection: "Insurance does not cover this." Correct, and that is actually an advantage for most people. Traditional hearing aids cost $3,200 or more, and even with insurance, you pay $1,500 to $3,000 out of pocket after deductibles and coverage limits. Medicare Part B does not cover hearing aids at all. WHSPR Assist at $249 to $349 is less than most hearing aid copays. No insurance paperwork, no pre-authorization, no coverage denial. HSA and FSA accounts typically cover assistive technology for pre-tax purchasing.

Ninth objection: "What about privacy with the cameras?" The cameras activate only when you tell them to — a tap on the temple arm or a voice command. There is no continuous recording, no cloud upload of video, and no facial recognition database. The AI processes locally on the device for text reading and scene description. A visible LED indicator lights up when the camera is active, so people around you know. This is the same privacy model used by Ray-Ban Meta glasses, which have been on the market since 2023.

Tenth objection: "What happens when they break or need repair?" TR thermoplastic frames are among the most durable materials in eyewear — they flex, resist impact, and return to shape. If something does break, the clinic handles the warranty exchange the same way they handle any frame issue. At $249 to $349, the replacement cost is a fraction of repairing a $4,000 hearing aid. Software updates happen automatically through the companion app.

**Credibility Proof Points.** WHSPR Assist builds credibility through seven reinforcing channels. Professional endorsement from trusted audiologists and optometrists transfers their clinical credibility directly. Clinical infrastructure — the Provider Portal, audiogram calibration, hearing profile tracking, and remote recalibration — signals clinical seriousness. Bluetooth 5.4 exceeds every hearing aid on the market, including Phonak's 5.3. The 32MP AI camera delivers vision-assistance capabilities comparable to $4,500 OrCam devices at roughly one-thirteenth the price. Dual-mic ANC/ENC is the same technology used in professional conference headsets. TR frames are used by premium optical brands. And the price itself — $249 to $349 — occupies a credibility sweet spot: not suspiciously cheap (a $29 price would trigger quality concerns) and not prohibitively expensive (a $1,500 price would trigger comparison to hearing aids).

### Feelings

WHSPR Assist targets six specific emotional responses, ordered by priority.

Dignity is the master emotion. "Nobody can tell I am wearing anything special. They just see my glasses." Every design decision, every marketing message, every clinical interaction must preserve and reinforce the wearer's self-image. The product does not "help" someone who is broken. It enhances someone who is complete.

Quiet confidence is the second emotion. Hearing without pretending. No more leaning in, no more asking people to repeat themselves, no more guessing what was said and hoping the response fits.

Reconnection is third. Being part of the circle again — following the conversation at the dinner table, hearing the joke, responding in time.

Relief is fourth. Relief from the difficulty itself, and relief from the stigma of addressing it. Relief is what Sarah the caregiver feels, too, when she sees her dad wearing them and actually engaging.

Independence is fifth. Reading the prescription bottle without asking for help. Navigating the grocery store without a magnifying app. Staying self-sufficient in the small moments that add up.

Trust is sixth. The professional recommendation enables the purchase. Trust in the audiologist transfers to trust in the product. This is why the B2B channel is not just a distribution strategy — it is an emotional strategy.

The anti-emotions are equally defined. WHSPR Assist must never make anyone feel pitied, old, broken, pressured, patronized, or guilty.

### Resonance

The product achieves resonance through several mechanisms. It becomes a daily essential — the wearer replaces their previous glasses, and WHSPR Assist becomes the first thing they put on in the morning. Returns increase over time as the AI learns preferences and the wearer discovers new use cases. Clinical continuity via the Provider Portal creates an ongoing relationship between the wearer and their clinician. The upgrade path from $249 glasses to $4,000 hearing aids is natural and unconflicted because the product was never positioned as a replacement. Network effects emerge as wearers recommend the product to friends and family. And the brand mission holds it all together: "No one should miss a conversation because of stigma or cost."

**Points of Parity.** WHSPR Assist matches or exceeds expectations in seven areas: it accepts standard Rx lens grinding from any optometrist, it is comfortable for all-day wear, it improves speech audibility in noise, it offers audiogram calibration through the companion app, it connects to smartphones via BT 5.4, it includes a dual camera AI system, and it uses the latest Bluetooth standard.

**Points of Difference.** Six attributes separate WHSPR Assist from everything else on the market: it is the only product combining hearing enhancement, vision correction, and AI assistance in a single frame at any price; it is the only Rx smart glasses designed specifically for adults 50 and older; it is the only hearing glasses distributed through both audiologists and optometrists; it represents a 90 to 95 percent cost reduction versus traditional hearing aids; it offers a zero-stigma form factor; and it provides full whitelabel capability that no competitor offers.

**Value Logic.** WHSPR Assist competes against doing nothing, not against hearing aids. Eighty percent of the target market currently chooses inaction. The value proposition is not "$249 versus $4,000" — it is "$249 versus $0." For $249 to $349, the wearer adds hearing enhancement, AI vision assistance, and smart features to the glasses they already wear, in a zero-stigma form factor.

**Competitive Notes.** Against Phonak, the comparison is $299 versus $3,200, and WHSPR captures the 60 percent who decline traditional hearing aids entirely. Against OrCam, WHSPR provides comparable AI vision at one-thirteenth the price, plus hearing enhancement. Against Ray-Ban Meta, WHSPR offers prescription lenses and dedicated hearing enhancement that Meta does not, with a 12-to-24-month competitive window before Meta could respond to the clinical distribution model.

## Voice and Tone

### The Voice Persona — Dr. Maren

WHSPR Assist speaks as Dr. Maren — a composite persona, not a character that appears in marketing. She is not named in copy. She is the invisible author behind every word.

Dr. Maren is 52 years old. She is an optometrist who grew up in a small town where her grandmother wore hearing aids for twenty years. She watched her grandmother shrink from a confident woman into someone who apologized before every sentence. That experience shaped her career. She went into vision care, but she never stopped thinking about hearing. When prescription smart glasses arrived that could do both, she felt something click into place.

Maren explains things the way she would explain them to her own mother — clearly, completely, without fuss. She never talks down. She never oversimplifies. She respects that the people she serves have lived full lives and made thousands of good decisions. She does not assume they need help thinking. She assumes they need better information and better tools.

She never pities. She never rushes. She never uses clinical jargon outside of clinical contexts. She never says "easy" because she knows that adopting new technology is not easy for everyone, and pretending otherwise is disrespectful. Her default register is calm, specific, and grounded. She prefers concrete outcomes ("hear every word at dinner") over abstract promises ("experience better hearing"). She leads with what the product does, not what the person lacks.

### Three Calibrated Tones

**Compassionate Companion** is the B2C end-user voice, written for George (55-75). Formality scores 4, warmth scores 8, authority scores 5. Reading level is Grade 6-7. Average sentence length is 12 to 16 words, maximum 22 words. Paragraphs are 2 to 3 sentences. The tonal source is AARP Magazine meets Consumer Reports — informative, calm, evidence-aware, never breathless or promotional. This voice leads with outcomes George cares about: hearing grandchildren, reading menus, enjoying dinner out. It references trusted figures: "your audiologist," "your optometrist." It uses concrete scenarios: "at the restaurant," "on the golf course," "at your granddaughter's recital."

**Peer Professional** is the B2B clinician voice, written for Dr. Priya. Formality scores 7, warmth scores 4, authority scores 8. Reading level is Grade 10-12. Average sentence length is 16 to 22 words, maximum 30 words. Paragraphs are 3 to 5 sentences. The tonal source is JAMA meets product brief — clinical competence without academic pretension. This voice uses clinical vocabulary where it serves clarity: "mild-to-moderate hearing enhancement," "audiometric profile," "patient compliance," "fitting protocol." It leads with practice outcomes: patient retention, revenue per declined patient, competitive differentiation.

**Trusted Advisor** is the B2C caregiver voice, written for Sarah (35-55). Formality scores 5, warmth scores 7, authority scores 6. Reading level is Grade 7-8. Average sentence length is 14 to 18 words, maximum 25 words. Paragraphs are 2 to 4 sentences. This voice validates the caregiver's experience: "You've watched him pretend to hear. You've sent the links. You've made the appointment he didn't keep." It leads with the parent's dignity: "He'll wear these because they look like his regular glasses." It addresses the gift pathway: affordable enough to give as a gift, meaningful enough to matter.

### Language Standards — Always Use

The following substitutions are mandatory across all communications. Say "hear more clearly" instead of "hearing loss solution." Say "see more sharply" instead of "vision impairment aid." Say "smart glasses" instead of "device." Say "hearing enhancement" instead of "correction" or "treatment." Say "your audiologist" or "your optometrist" instead of "a doctor." Say "designed for your life" instead of "designed for seniors." Say "straightforward" instead of "easy" or "simple." Say "prescription-ready" instead of any clinical device language. The governing principle is always: clarity, connection, confidence.

### Language Standards — Never Use

These words and phrases are banned from all WHSPR Assist communications across all three voices: hearing loss solution, disability, impairment, elderly, anti-aging, despite your age, suffer from, struggle with, game-changing, revolutionary, disruptive, "don't miss another moment" (implies they are already missing life), "before it's too late" (fear-based urgency), "you deserve" (implies deprivation, feels like pity), easy, simple, senior, old, patient (in B2C copy), device (use "smart glasses"), fix, cure, treat.

### Six Constitutional Anti-Patterns

These are the six things WHSPR Assist must never do in any communication, any channel, any voice. They are constitutional. Violation of any anti-pattern requires rewriting.

Anti-Pattern One is condescension and infantilization. It looks like: "Just follow these three easy steps!" or "Don't worry, it's so simple!" The fix is: "Here is how to get started." Write clearly without writing down. Assume intelligence. Simplify language, not ideas.

Anti-Pattern Two is age-qualifying language. It looks like: "For someone your age, this is remarkable technology." The fix is: "thoughtfully designed." Never reference age unless demographics are structurally required. Address the person, not the cohort.

Anti-Pattern Three is medical outcome promises. It looks like: "Restore your hearing to normal." The fix is: "Hear more clearly in noisy rooms." Promise enhancement, not restoration. Making medical claims invites FDA scrutiny, erodes audiologist trust, and sets expectations the product cannot meet.

Anti-Pattern Four is fear and urgency tactics. It looks like: "Untreated hearing loss leads to cognitive decline." The fix is: "Enjoy dinner out again." Fear-based marketing activates the exact avoidance mechanism that keeps George from acting. Research consistently shows that fear messaging reduces hearing aid adoption, not increases it.

Anti-Pattern Five is disability framing. It looks like: "For people with hearing disabilities." The fix is: "people who want to hear more clearly." The word "disability" triggers stigma, shame, and identity threat. It is the verbal equivalent of a visible hearing aid.

Anti-Pattern Six is visual dependency tropes. It looks like stock photos of confused elderly people squinting at phones, or old hands cupping ears, or grandparents looking lost while family talks around them. The fix is: show capable adults doing things they enjoy — golfing, reading, dining, playing with grandchildren — with clear eyes and confident posture. The glasses should be visible but unremarkable. The person wearing them should look engaged, not rescued.

### Sentence Construction Rules

Active voice is mandatory. One idea per sentence. Front-load the benefit — the first five words of every sentence should contain the value. Specificity over abstraction: "Hear your granddaughter's joke at the birthday table" is better than "experience improved hearing in social settings." No hedge words in headlines. Numbers are powerful: "$249" is more persuasive than "affordable." Questions are invitations only — use rhetorical questions sparingly to open sections, never questions that could be answered with "no."

## Visual Identity System

### Visual Mission and Design Philosophy

The visual mission of WHSPR Assist is to project clinical credibility fused with human warmth. Every visual element must pass the Dignity Test: would George feel respected seeing this?

The design philosophy rests on three pillars. Clinical Warmth means the brand looks trustworthy enough for a doctor's office but warm enough for a kitchen table. Dignity-First Design means every visual choice preserves the self-image of the wearer. Accessible Premium means the brand signals quality without signaling luxury or clinical sterility.

The visual territory is defined by what it is and what it is not. It is warm clinical and clean plus warm. It is not sterile clinical, tech-bro, patronizing senior, or budget OTC.

### Color System

The color palette is divided into primary, secondary, and extended colors, each with precise specifications for consistent reproduction across all media.

**Trust Navy** is the anchor color, used at 60 percent weight for backgrounds, headers, and clinical credibility signaling. The hex value is #1B365D. RGB values are 27, 54, 93. CMYK values are 90, 62, 24, 27. The Pantone reference is 289 C.

**Warm Linen** provides warmth at 30 percent weight, used for text backgrounds and breathing space. The hex value is #F5F0E8. RGB values are 245, 240, 232. CMYK values are 3, 4, 8, 0. The Pantone reference is 7527 C.

**Clarity Teal** is the action color at 10 percent weight, used for calls to action and hearing-related feature highlights. The hex value is #2A9D8F. RGB values are 42, 157, 143. CMYK values are 73, 0, 47, 0. The Pantone reference is 7472 C.

**Soft Silver** serves as the neutral support color for borders, separators, and device background contexts. The hex value is #B0BEC5. RGB values are 176, 190, 197. CMYK values are 28, 13, 9, 6. The Pantone reference is Cool Gray 4 C.

**Vitality Amber** is the accent indicator color for active states, badges, and highlights. The hex value is #E9A825. RGB values are 233, 168, 37. CMYK values are 7, 33, 94, 0. The Pantone reference is 7408 C.

The extended palette includes five additional values. Charcoal Ink (#2C3E50) is the primary body text color. Slate Gray (#5D6D7E) handles secondary text and captions. Cloud White (#FAFAF8) is the default page background. Mist Gray (#E0E4E8) defines dividers and section separators. Deep Navy (#0F1F33) anchors footers and dark mode interfaces.

### Typography

The type system uses three families, each chosen for functional and emotional reasons.

**Source Serif 4** is the header typeface. It carries Dr. Maren's calm authority through transitional letterforms — neither starkly modern nor nostalgically traditional. H1 headings are set at 40 to 56 pixels in SemiBold weight. H2 headings are 32 to 40 pixels. H3 headings are 24 to 32 pixels in Regular weight. Pull quotes and featured text use 24 to 28 pixels. The rationale for Source Serif 4 is that its transitional design connotes trustworthiness and professionalism without clinical coldness.

**Source Sans 3** is the body typeface. Primary body text is set at 18 to 20 pixels. Compact body text (captions, secondary information) is 16 to 18 pixels. The minimum text size for any WHSPR communication is 16 pixels, reflecting the 55-to-75 age demographic.

**IBM Plex Mono** is the data typeface, used for specifications, measurements, technical comparisons, and the Provider Portal interface.

### Brand Sigil and Logo

The WHSPR sigil combines three visual elements: a sound wave arc representing hearing enhancement, a lens circle representing prescription vision, and a connection dot representing the AI and connectivity layer. The sigil is constructed on a 48 by 48 pixel grid with a 1.2 to 1 aspect ratio.

Four logo versions exist for different contexts. The primary horizontal lockup places the sigil alongside the wordmark. The stacked version places the sigil above the wordmark for square applications. The sigil-only version is used when the brand name appears elsewhere in the layout. The wordmark-only version is used in text-heavy contexts where the sigil would be too small. Minimum reproduction sizes are 160 pixels for the horizontal digital lockup and 32 pixels for the standalone sigil.

### Photography Direction

Photography for WHSPR Assist follows specific technical and editorial guidelines. The recommended camera is a Canon R5 with a 50mm lens at f/2.8. Color temperature should range from 5200K to 5800K for warm, natural light. Color grading should be warm with lifted shadows — never cold, never harsh.

Editorial direction prioritizes specific scenarios: a family dinner with multiple generations, a morning routine reading a prescription bottle, coffee with a friend, a clinical handoff moment between provider and patient, garden reading, golf, grocery store independence, and the moment of a prescription recommendation. These scenarios are selected because they represent the actual moments where WHSPR Assist delivers value.

Photography must never show struggling or confused elderly people, isolation or sadness, cold or blue color temperatures, or a product without human context. The person wearing the glasses should always look engaged, capable, and naturally confident.

### Illustration Style

Illustrations follow a clean, medical-grade line style. Lines are 2 pixels in weight with round caps and round joins. Primary colors for illustration are Trust Navy and Charcoal Ink. Clarity Teal is used at 20 to 40 percent opacity for fills. Maximum three colors per illustration, no gradients.

Subject matter for illustrations includes how-it-works diagrams, feature callout graphics, clinical workflow sequences, and step-by-step guides. Illustrations must never include cartoons, mascots, decorative elements, isometric perspectives, or anime-style art.

### Graphic Elements and Component System

The Sound Wave Pattern is a repeating arc motif used as a background texture at 5 to 8 percent opacity, tiled at 96 by 96 pixels. It provides subtle brand reinforcement without visual noise.

Cards use 12-pixel border radius, 24-pixel internal padding, and a subtle drop shadow. Buttons come in three tiers: primary buttons use Clarity Teal (#2A9D8F) as the fill color, secondary buttons use Trust Navy as an outlined stroke, and tertiary buttons use teal-colored text only. Badges distinguish between prescription content (Vitality Amber) and feature highlights (Clarity Teal).

The spacing scale uses a 4-pixel base unit. Extra-small spacing is 4 pixels. Small is 8 pixels. Medium is 16 pixels. Large is 24 pixels. Extra-large is 32 pixels. 2XL is 48 pixels. 3XL is 64 pixels. 4XL is 96 pixels.

The grid system uses 12 columns at desktop width (1120-pixel maximum content width), 8 columns at tablet width (720-pixel maximum), and 4 columns at mobile width.

### Accessibility Standards

All WHSPR Assist visual communications must meet WCAG 2.1 AA as the minimum standard. The minimum text size is 16 pixels, with 18 pixels preferred for the primary 55-to-75 audience. Minimum touch targets are 44 pixels for web and 48 pixels for the companion app.

Key contrast ratios have been validated. Trust Navy (#1B365D) on Warm Linen (#F5F0E8) achieves a contrast ratio of 10.2 to 1, passing AAA. Clarity Teal (#2A9D8F) on light backgrounds fails AA for normal-sized text — it must be used at a minimum of 18 pixels or as a background color with Trust Navy text to maintain readability. Vitality Amber (#E9A825) on Trust Navy achieves a contrast ratio of 4.7 to 1 and is approved for badges only, not body text.

### Application Contexts

For the website, Cloud White (#FAFAF8) serves as the body background. Trust Navy anchors the hero section. Charcoal Ink (#2C3E50) is the body text color. Clarity Teal powers all calls to action.

For the companion app, white cards float on a light background. IBM Plex Mono displays data and measurements. Touch targets are a minimum of 48 pixels.

For clinical brochures, Warm Linen is printed on uncoated 100gsm stock. Imagery is reproduced at 300dpi minimum.

For packaging, Warm Linen stock is the base material. Trust Navy is debossed on the exterior. The Sound Wave Pattern lines the interior as a brand-reinforcing detail.

For email, layouts use a 600-pixel maximum width. Trust Navy anchors the header. White backgrounds hold the body content. Clarity Teal calls to action are sized at a minimum of 48 pixels in height.

### Visual Negative Prompt

When generating visual assets for WHSPR Assist — whether through AI tools, stock photography, or commissioned work — the following elements must be explicitly excluded: patronizing elderly imagery, walkers or wheelchairs unless directly relevant to the content, isolation or sadness, sterile white clinical environments, cold fluorescent lighting, generic stock elderly people, tech-bro aesthetics, young models wearing the product, gaming or creator contexts, anime, cartoon, pixel art, low poly, vaporwave, glitch art, hearing aid stigma imagery, disability-focused framing, excessive chrome, neon, cyberpunk, synthwave, products floating in white space with specification callouts, before-and-after deficit comparisons, extreme ear close-ups, dutch angles, forced perspective, heavy gradients, lens flares, and HDR over-processing.
