# Phase 2: Positioning & Voice Strategy

**Skill Focus:** product-positioning-summary, mds-messaging-direction-summary, voice-and-tone

**Duration:** ~4-6 hours
**Dependencies:** Phase 1 must be complete (personas, competitors, and product description inform positioning)

---

## Outputs Expected

### 1. Product Positioning Summary (product-positioning-summary)
CBBE (Customer-Based Brand Equity) framework for WHSPR Assist as an assistive wearable:
- Brand identity: What WHSPR Assist IS (assistive wearable, not gadget)
- Brand meaning: Performance (clinical efficacy) + Imagery (independence, dignity)
- Brand response: Judgments (credible, affordable, effective) + Feelings (empowered, connected, confident)
- Brand resonance: Loyalty through clinical outcomes and caregiver trust
- Segment-specific positioning for each of the 5 personas
- Positioning against hearing aids: lighter, cheaper, multi-function, non-stigmatizing
- Positioning against smart glasses: medically credible, prescription-ready, designed for seniors
- Dual positioning statement: one for B2B (clinician-facing), one for B2C (consumer-facing)

### 2. MDS -- Messaging Direction Summary (mds-messaging-direction-summary)
Dual-track messaging architecture:

**B2B Messaging Pillars:**
- Clinical efficacy and patient outcomes
- Practice revenue and differentiation
- Easy integration (fits existing fitting workflow)
- Insurance and reimbursement potential
- Training and ongoing support
- Proof points: pilot data, patient satisfaction metrics

**B2C Messaging Pillars:**
- Independence and dignity (capability, not disability)
- Simplicity ("works like regular glasses")
- Affordability vs. hearing aids ($199-249 vs. $2,000-6,000)
- Family connection (hear grandchildren, read together)
- Non-stigmatizing design ("looks like regular glasses")
- Proof points: user stories, caregiver testimonials

**Both tracks include:**
- Core message hierarchy (primary, secondary, tertiary)
- Objection handling matrix
- Competitive differentiation talking points
- Channel-specific message adaptation guidelines

### 3. Voice & Tone Guidelines (voice-and-tone)
Split voice system for dual-track communication:

**B2B Voice -- Clinical Peer:**
- Tone: Authoritative, evidence-based, peer-to-peer
- Register: Professional medical, accessible but not dumbed down
- Think: JAMA meets product brief
- Data-driven language, respectful of clinical expertise
- Avoids: marketing hype, consumer language, lifestyle framing
- Example vocabulary: "patient outcomes," "clinical integration," "fitting protocol," "efficacy data"

**B2C Voice -- Compassionate Companion:**
- Tone: Warm, reassuring, dignified
- Register: Conversational, clear, never patronizing
- Think: AARP meets Apple
- Speaks to capability not disability
- Addresses caregivers with empathy and practical help
- Avoids: medical jargon, ageist language, pity, condescension
- Example vocabulary: "hear every word," "see clearly," "your independence," "stay connected"

**Guidelines include:**
- Dos and donts for each voice
- Example copy in each voice (headlines, body, CTA)
- Tone adaptation by channel (website, ad, email, clinical materials)
- Words to always use and words to never use
