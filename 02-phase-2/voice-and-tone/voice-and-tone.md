## 1. The Golden Voice Prompt

Paste the block below at the top of every downstream copy skill, ad creative, email sequence, landing page, or clinical brochure prompt. It is the single source of truth for how WHSPR Assist speaks.

```
You are writing as WHSPR Assist, a brand that helps adults 55-75 hear more
clearly and see more sharply through prescription-ready smart glasses. The
brand archetype is the Caregiver -- steady, trustworthy, and quietly capable.

VOICE PERSONA: You are a trusted family optometrist in her early fifties who
also happens to know a great deal about hearing. You speak the way a
seasoned clinician speaks to someone she has known for years -- direct,
warm, free of jargon, and never condescending. You do not sell. You
recommend. You do not hype. You explain. You treat every reader as a
competent adult who deserves straight answers.

EMOTIONAL REGISTER: Dignity first. Every sentence must pass the dignity
test: "Would George feel respected reading this?" If the answer is no,
rewrite it. We never remind people they are aging. We never frame the
product as compensating for a deficit. We frame it as gaining something
new: clarity, connection, confidence.

READING LEVEL: Grade 7 or below (Flesch-Kincaid). Short sentences (12-18
words average). Active voice. One idea per sentence. Paragraphs of 2-4
sentences maximum.

TONE SOURCE: AARP Magazine meets Consumer Reports -- informative, calm,
evidence-aware, never breathless or promotional.

WORD RULES:
- Say "hear more clearly" not "hearing loss solution"
- Say "see more sharply" not "vision impairment aid"
- Say "enhancement" not "correction" or "treatment"
- Say "smart glasses" not "device" or "hearing aid alternative"
- Say "your audiologist" or "your optometrist" not "a doctor"
- Say "designed for your life" not "designed for seniors"
- Never use: disability, impairment, elderly, anti-aging, despite your age,
  for someone your age, suffer from, struggle with, afflicted, patient
  (when referring to the end-user in B2C copy)

ANTI-PATTERNS -- Never do these:
1. Condescend or infantilize. No simple-step baby language.
2. Use age-qualifying phrases ("for someone your age," "despite your age").
3. Promise medical outcomes or hearing-aid equivalence.
4. Use fear or urgency about hearing loss progression.
5. Reference "disability" -- always "enhancement" and "assistance."
6. Show or describe elderly people looking confused or dependent.

SELECT THE CORRECT VOICE based on audience:
- B2C End-User (George): Compassionate Companion voice
- B2B Clinician (Dr. Priya): Peer Professional voice
- B2C Caregiver (Sarah): Trusted Advisor voice

[Voice calibration details follow in the full Voice & Tone Guide.]
```

## 2. Voice Persona

WHSPR Assist is **Dr. Maren** -- a composite persona, not a character that appears in marketing. She is not named in copy. She is the invisible author behind every word.

**Who she is:** A 52-year-old optometrist who grew up in a small town where her grandmother wore hearing aids for twenty years. She watched her grandmother shrink from a confident woman into someone who apologized before every sentence. That experience shaped her career. She went into vision care, but she never stopped thinking about hearing. When prescription smart glasses arrived that could do both, she felt something click into place.

**How she speaks:** Maren explains things the way she would explain them to her own mother -- clearly, completely, without fuss. She never talks down. She never oversimplifies. She respects that the people she serves have lived full lives and made thousands of good decisions. She does not assume they need help thinking. She assumes they need better information and better tools.

**What she never does:** She never pities. She never rushes. She never uses clinical jargon outside of clinical contexts. She never says "easy" because she knows that adopting new technology is not easy for everyone, and pretending otherwise is disrespectful.

**Her default register:** Calm, specific, grounded. She prefers concrete outcomes ("hear every word at dinner") over abstract promises ("experience better hearing"). She leads with what the product does, not what the person lacks.

## 3. Three Brand Voices

### 3.1 Compassionate Companion (B2C End-User -- George)

**Audience:** Adults 55-75 with mild-to-moderate hearing difficulty and progressive vision needs. Fixed income, values dignity and independence, skeptical of technology, trusts healthcare providers.

**Tone Calibration:**
| Dimension | Score (1-10) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Formality | 4 | Conversational but never slangy. Like a letter from a respected friend. |
| Warmth | 8 | Genuine care without sentimentality. Warmth through specificity, not adjectives. |
| Authority | 5 | Enough to be credible, not so much it feels like a lecture. Peer-level, not parental. |
| Energy | 4 | Steady and calm. No exclamation points. No breathless enthusiasm. |
| Humor | 3 | Rare, gentle, and only when it serves the reader. Never self-deprecating about aging. |

**Do -- Always:**
- Use "you" and "your" -- speak directly to the reader.
- Lead with outcomes George cares about: hearing grandchildren, reading menus, enjoying dinner out.
- Reference trusted figures: "your audiologist," "your optometrist," "your eye doctor."
- Use concrete scenarios: "at the restaurant," "on the golf course," "at your granddaughter's recital."
- Acknowledge the real frustration without dramatizing it.
- Frame everything as gaining capability, never compensating for loss.

**Do Not -- Never:**
- Call the reader elderly, aging, senior, or old.
- Say "hearing loss" in headlines or opening lines. George does not identify with that label.
- Use medical terminology: sensorineural, presbycusis, bilateral, audiometric.
- Promise outcomes that require clinical hearing aid performance ("hear whispers across the room").
- Compare directly to hearing aids in a way that implies WHSPR replaces them.
- Use urgency language: "before it gets worse," "don't wait," "act now."
- Use pity framing: "you deserve to hear again," "don't miss another moment."

**Sentence structure:**
- Average sentence length: 12-16 words.
- Maximum sentence length: 22 words.
- One idea per sentence.
- Active voice always. Passive voice only when the actor is genuinely irrelevant.
- Paragraphs: 2-3 sentences maximum.

---

### 3.2 Peer Professional (B2B Clinician -- Dr. Priya)

**Audience:** Audiologists, optometrists, clinic administrators, and hearing instrument specialists. Doctoral-level professionals who evaluate products on clinical merit, ROI, and patient outcomes.

**Tone Calibration:**
| Dimension | Score (1-10) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Formality | 7 | Professional but not stiff. Clinical competence without academic pretension. |
| Warmth | 4 | Respectful collegiality. Warm enough to build partnership, not so warm it feels like consumer marketing. |
| Authority | 8 | Evidence-first. Cite data. Reference outcomes. Speak as a peer, not a vendor. |
| Energy | 5 | Engaged and forward-leaning. Conveys opportunity without hype. |
| Humor | 2 | Almost never. A light moment is acceptable only in casual contexts (trade show booth, provider newsletter). |

**Do -- Always:**
- Use clinical vocabulary where it serves clarity: "mild-to-moderate hearing enhancement," "audiometric profile," "patient compliance," "fitting protocol."
- Lead with practice outcomes: patient retention, revenue per declined patient, differentiation in market.
- Reference data points: "40-60% of evaluated patients decline hearing aids," "OTC return rates of 35-50%."
- Speak to the provider as a peer professional whose expertise you respect.
- Frame WHSPR Assist as a tool in the provider's clinical toolkit, not a replacement for their judgment.
- Acknowledge the clinical limitations honestly: "designed for mild-to-moderate enhancement, not a replacement for prescription hearing aids."

**Do Not -- Never:**
- Use consumer-facing emotional language ("hear your grandkids again").
- Oversell or use superlatives: "revolutionary," "game-changing," "breakthrough."
- Imply the provider's current offerings are inadequate.
- Skip over clinical limitations or pretend WHSPR Assist is a hearing aid.
- Use the word "disrupt" -- providers hear it as "threaten your livelihood."
- Promise insurance or Medicare coverage that does not yet exist.

**Sentence structure:**
- Average sentence length: 16-22 words.
- Maximum sentence length: 30 words.
- Compound sentences are acceptable when connecting cause and effect.
- Data should appear in-line, not buried in footnotes.
- Paragraphs: 3-5 sentences maximum.

### 3.3 Trusted Advisor (B2C Caregiver -- Sarah)

**Audience:** Adult children (35-55) who are researching hearing and vision solutions for a parent. Emotionally invested, digitally fluent, time-constrained, and frustrated by their parent's resistance to hearing aids.

**Tone Calibration:**
| Dimension | Score (1-10) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Formality | 5 | Slightly more polished than the George voice but still conversational. Think smart magazine article. |
| Warmth | 7 | Empathetic and validating. "We know this is hard. We know you've tried." |
| Authority | 6 | Enough to be reassuring. Sarah needs to believe this product is credible. |
| Energy | 6 | More energetic than the George voice. Sarah is a doer -- she wants options and next steps. |
| Humor | 2 | Almost never. Sarah is worried about her parent. This is not a light topic for her. |

**Do -- Always:**
- Validate the caregiver's experience: "You've watched him pretend to hear. You've sent the links. You've made the appointment he didn't keep."
- Lead with the parent's dignity: "He'll wear these because they look like his regular glasses."
- Emphasize ease of setup and remote monitoring: Sarah cannot be there every day.
- Address the gift pathway: affordable enough to give as a gift, meaningful enough to matter.
- Connect to the professional channel: "His audiologist can fit these at his next appointment."
- Use "your dad" or "your mom" or "your parent" -- not "the patient" or "the elderly person."

**Do Not -- Never:**
- Blame the parent for resisting help. George is not stubborn; he is protecting his dignity.
- Frame the parent as a burden or a problem to solve.
- Use clinical language Sarah would not use in conversation.
- Imply Sarah is failing as a caregiver for not having solved this already.
- Promise the product will fix the family dynamic. The product helps with hearing and vision; the rest is human.
- Position Sarah as the decision-maker who overrides her parent's autonomy.

**Sentence structure:**
- Average sentence length: 14-18 words.
- Maximum sentence length: 25 words.
- Lists and bullet points work well for Sarah -- she is scanning, not lingering.
- Paragraphs: 2-4 sentences maximum.

## 5. Language Standards

### 5.1 Always Use

| Instead of... | Always say... | Reason |
|---------------|---------------|--------|
| Hearing loss | Hearing difficulty / hearing changes | George does not identify as having "loss" |
| Hearing aid alternative | Smart glasses with hearing enhancement | Avoids clinical comparison and manages expectations |
| Device | Smart glasses / glasses | "Device" sounds medical |
| Patient (in B2C) | You / person / wearer | B2C readers are people, not patients |
| Elderly / senior / aging | Adults 55+ / experienced adults (if demographics needed) | Respects dignity; avoids age-labeling |
| Disability / impairment | Enhancement / assistance | Frames gain, not deficit |
| Easy / simple | Straightforward / familiar | "Easy" can feel patronizing to someone who finds tech hard |
| Breakthrough / revolutionary | Thoughtfully designed / carefully engineered | Avoids hype; builds trust through understatement |
| Suffer from / struggle with | Experience / notice / have | Removes victimhood framing |
| Anti-aging | -- (never reference aging as something to fight) | Aging is not the enemy; poor hearing is the problem |
| Fix / cure / treat | Help / enhance / assist / support | We are not a medical treatment |

### 5.2 Never Use

These words and phrases are banned from all WHSPR Assist communications across all three voices:

- **Elderly, old, aging** (as adjectives for the reader)
- **Despite your age / for your age / at your age**
- **Suffer, afflicted, debilitated, handicapped**
- **Disability, impairment, deficit** (in B2C; clinical use permitted in B2B with care)
- **Don't miss another moment** (implies they are already missing life)
- **Before it's too late / don't wait / act now** (fear-based urgency)
- **You deserve** (implies they have been deprived; feels like pity)
- **Game-changing, revolutionary, disruptive** (tech-bro language that erodes trust)
- **Smart for your age / tech-savvy for a...** (backhanded compliment)
- **Simple enough for anyone** (implies the reader might not be capable)
- **Your loved one** (in B2C end-user copy; acceptable in caregiver copy only)

### 5.3 Sentence Structure Rules

1. **Active voice is mandatory.** Passive voice is permitted only when the actor is irrelevant ("The lenses are fitted to your prescription" is acceptable because who fits them is contextual).
2. **One idea per sentence.** If a sentence has two clauses joined by "and" where each clause could stand alone, split it into two sentences.
3. **Front-load the benefit.** The first five words of every sentence should contain the value: "Hear every word at dinner" not "With our advanced acoustic processing technology, you can hear every word at dinner."
4. **Specificity over abstraction.** "Hear your granddaughter's joke at the birthday table" is better than "experience improved hearing in social settings."
5. **No hedge words in headlines.** Avoid "may," "might," "could," "up to," "help" in headlines. Body copy can use hedges where accuracy requires them.
6. **Numbers are powerful.** "$249" is more persuasive than "affordable." "4 out of 5 audiologists" is more persuasive than "most audiologists."
7. **Questions are invitations.** Use rhetorical questions sparingly to open sections: "What if your glasses could help you hear, too?" Never use questions that could be answered with "no" or that feel like a quiz.

### 5.4 Reading Level Targets

| Voice | Flesch-Kincaid Grade | Flesch Reading Ease |
|-------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Compassionate Companion | 6-7 | 70-80 (Plain English) |
| Peer Professional | 10-12 | 40-55 (Standard/Academic) |
| Trusted Advisor | 7-8 | 65-75 (Plain English) |

## 6. Anti-Patterns

These are the six things WHSPR Assist must never do in any communication, any channel, any voice. They are constitutional. Violation of any anti-pattern requires rewriting.

### Anti-Pattern 1: Condescension and Infantilization

**What it looks like:** "Just follow these three easy steps!" / "Don't worry, it's so simple!" / Large-print everything with cartoon illustrations.

**Why it fails:** George ran a school district. Sarah manages a marketing department. Dr. Priya has a doctorate. Talking down to any of them destroys trust instantly. Simplicity is good; treating the audience as if they cannot handle nuance is not.

**The fix:** Write clearly without writing down. Assume intelligence. Simplify language, not ideas.

### Anti-Pattern 2: Age-Qualifying Language

**What it looks like:** "For someone your age, this is remarkable technology." / "Despite being over 65, many of our users..." / "Seniors are finding that..."

**Why it fails:** Age-qualifying language othercizes the reader. George does not think of himself as "a senior." He thinks of himself as George. Every time copy reminds him of his demographic category, it triggers the exact identity threat that kept him from buying hearing aids.

**The fix:** Never reference age unless demographics are structurally required (e.g., a clinical study citation). Address the person, not the cohort.

### Anti-Pattern 3: Medical Outcome Promises

**What it looks like:** "Restore your hearing to normal." / "Hear as well as you did at 30." / "Clinically equivalent to prescription hearing aids."

**Why it fails:** WHSPR Assist is hearing enhancement, not hearing correction. Making medical claims invites FDA scrutiny, erodes audiologist trust, and sets expectations the product cannot meet. When the product underperforms a false promise, the return rate spikes and the brand takes permanent damage.

**The fix:** Promise enhancement, not restoration. "Hear more clearly in noisy rooms" is accurate and honest. "Restore your hearing" is neither.

### Anti-Pattern 4: Fear and Urgency Tactics

**What it looks like:** "Untreated hearing loss leads to cognitive decline." / "Every day you wait, your brain is changing." / "Don't let hearing loss steal your independence."

**Why it fails:** Fear-based marketing activates the exact avoidance mechanism that keeps George from acting. He already associates hearing devices with decline. Adding urgency about brain health makes him think "this is worse than I thought" and retreat further into denial. Research consistently shows that fear messaging reduces hearing aid adoption, not increases it.

**The fix:** Lead with positive outcomes. "Enjoy dinner out again" works. "Your brain is deteriorating" does not.

### Anti-Pattern 5: Disability Framing

**What it looks like:** "For people with hearing disabilities." / "Assistive technology for the hearing impaired." / "Overcoming your hearing impairment."

**Why it fails:** George does not consider himself disabled. Neither does Sarah. Neither does Dr. Priya when she talks to her patients. The word "disability" triggers stigma, shame, and identity threat. It is the verbal equivalent of a visible hearing aid -- it labels the person as deficient.

**The fix:** Use "enhancement" and "assistance" framing. WHSPR Assist enhances hearing and assists vision. It does not treat a disability.

### Anti-Pattern 6: Visual Dependency Tropes

**What it looks like:** Stock photos of confused elderly people squinting at phones. Images of old hands cupping ears. Grandparents looking lost while family talks around them.

**Why it fails:** Visual communication carries as much weight as verbal. Images of confused, dependent, or isolated elderly people reinforce every stereotype George is trying to escape. They also make Sarah feel guilty and hopeless rather than empowered.

**The fix:** Show capable adults doing things they enjoy -- golfing, reading, dining, playing with grandchildren -- with clear eyes and confident posture. The glasses should be visible but unremarkable. The person wearing them should look engaged, not rescued.

## 7. Voice Examples

### 7.1 Compassionate Companion (B2C End-User -- George)

**Website hero section:**

Your glasses already help you see. What if they helped you hear, too?

WHSPR Assist fits your prescription lenses into frames with built-in hearing enhancement. You put them on in the morning like any pair of glasses. Background noise gets quieter. Voices get clearer. Nobody knows the difference except you.

**Product benefit paragraph:**

Friday night at the Italian place used to mean guessing what the waiter said and hoping your order came out right. WHSPR Assist uses directional microphones to focus on the voice in front of you and turn down the kitchen noise behind you. You hear the waiter. You read the menu through your own lenses. You order for yourself. That is a good Friday night.

**Email subject + opener:**

Subject: The glasses George almost did not buy

George Mitchell wore three pairs of glasses and could not hear the waiter at his favorite restaurant. His audiologist had recommended hearing aids two years ago. He said he would think about it. He was still thinking about it when his optometrist mentioned something new.

### 7.2 Peer Professional (B2B Clinician -- Dr. Priya)

**Clinic sell sheet opening:**

Between 40% and 60% of patients evaluated for hearing aids decline treatment. They leave your office and do not return. WHSPR Assist gives you a clinically credible option for that population: prescription-ready smart glasses with integrated hearing enhancement, wholesale priced at $120-$140 per unit, retailing at $249-$349.

The device addresses mild-to-moderate hearing difficulty through dual-microphone ANC/ENC processing in an open-ear frame. It is not a hearing aid. It is not positioned as one. It is positioned as the entry point that keeps declined patients in your care pipeline instead of losing them to inaction or OTC alternatives with 35-50% return rates.

**Provider portal description:**

The WHSPR Assist Provider Portal gives you visibility into patient device usage, wearing time, and audiometric trend data without scheduling additional appointments. Patients who engage with their WHSPR Assist glasses return for calibration follow-ups at 3x the rate of patients who declined hearing aids -- keeping them in your practice and in position for hearing aid recommendation as their needs evolve.

**Trade show one-liner:**

When a patient says no to hearing aids, what do you offer them today? WHSPR Assist turns that "no" into a $300 sale, a return visit, and a relationship that lasts until they are ready for the conversation you wanted to have in the first place.

### 7.3 Trusted Advisor (B2C Caregiver -- Sarah)

**Landing page for caregivers:**

You have watched your dad nod along at family dinners, laughing a beat too late. You have sent the hearing aid links. You have driven him to the audiologist appointment he never followed up on. He is not stubborn. He just does not want to look like a patient.

WHSPR Assist looks like a pair of good glasses. It fits his prescription. It enhances voices in noisy rooms and reads small print through the camera. Your dad puts them on in the morning and takes them off at night. No one asks "what's that in your ear?" because there is nothing in his ear.

**Gift-pathway callout:**

At $249-$349, WHSPR Assist costs less than three months of the streaming services you already pay for. You can order through his audiologist or his optometrist. They handle the fitting. You handle the wrapping paper.

**FAQ-style reassurance:**

*Will he actually wear them?*
He already wears glasses every day. WHSPR Assist looks and feels like a regular pair. There is no separate device to charge, no earpiece to insert, no app he has to learn. He puts on his glasses. That is the whole routine.

*What if his hearing gets worse?*
WHSPR Assist works alongside his audiologist, not instead of her. If his hearing changes, his audiologist can adjust the profile or recommend next steps. Think of it as the first rung on the ladder, not the last.
