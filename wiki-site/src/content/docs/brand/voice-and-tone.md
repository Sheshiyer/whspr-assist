# Voice & Tone Guide

> *Hear more. See more. Live more.*

---

## The Voice Persona: Dr. Maren

**Dr. Maren** is a 52-year-old optometrist who speaks like a trusted clinician talking to someone she has known for years. Direct, warm, free of jargon, never condescending. She recommends; she does not sell. She explains; she does not hype. She treats every reader as a competent adult who deserves straight answers.

### Core Culture

**Dignity-first.** Every word must pass the test: *Would George feel respected reading this?* If the answer is no, rewrite it.

- Enhancement framing always. Deficit framing never.
- Calm authority rooted in clinical credibility and human empathy.
- We never remind people they are aging.
- We never frame the product as compensating for a deficit.
- We frame it as gaining something new: **clarity, connection, confidence.**

## Language Rules

### Always Use

| Term | Context |
|------|---------|
| hear more clearly | Describing the hearing benefit |
| see more sharply | Describing the vision benefit |
| smart glasses | Product category name |
| hearing enhancement | What the product provides |
| your audiologist / your optometrist | Professional relationship framing |
| designed for your life | Lifestyle positioning |
| straightforward | Describing the experience |
| prescription-ready | Frame capability |
| clarity, connection, confidence | The three emotional pillars |

### Never Use

| Banned Term | Why |
|-------------|-----|
| hearing loss solution | Deficit framing |
| disability / impairment | Medical label |
| elderly / senior / old | Age-qualifying language |
| anti-aging / despite your age | Age-qualifying language |
| suffer from / struggle with | Victimhood framing |
| game-changing / revolutionary / disruptive | Hype language |
| don't miss another moment | Fear tactic |
| before it's too late | Urgency manipulation |
| you deserve | Entitlement framing |
| easy / simple | Condescending |
| patient (in B2C context) | Medical framing for consumers |
| device (use "smart glasses") | Clinical-device language |
| fix / cure / treat | Medical outcome promises |

## Target Emotions

Ordered by priority:

1. **Dignity** — the master emotion. Nobody can tell the wearer has anything special.
2. **Quiet confidence** — hearing the conversation without pretending.
3. **Reconnection** — being part of the circle again, especially at family events.
4. **Relief** — dual relief from functional difficulty and stigma of traditional solutions.
5. **Independence** — reading labels, navigating stores without asking for help.
6. **Trust** — professional recommendation as the emotional enabler.
7. **Competence** — feeling capable, not diminished.
8. **Belonging** — social participation restored.

### Anti-Emotions

Never make the reader feel: pitied, old, broken, pressured, patronized, or guilty.

## Anti-Patterns

### 1. Condescension and Infantilization

Never talk down to the reader. Assume intelligence. Simplify language, not ideas.

> ❌ "Just follow these three easy steps!"
> ✅ "Here is how to get started."

### 2. Age-Qualifying Language

Never reference age as a qualifier. Address the person, not the cohort.

> ❌ "For someone your age, this is remarkable technology."
> ✅ "This is thoughtfully designed technology."

### 3. Medical Outcome Promises

Never claim hearing aid equivalence or medical restoration. Promise enhancement, not restoration.

> ❌ "Restore your hearing to normal."
> ✅ "Hear more clearly in noisy rooms."

### 4. Fear and Urgency Tactics

Never use fear about hearing loss progression. Lead with positive outcomes.

> ❌ "Untreated hearing loss leads to cognitive decline."
> ✅ "Enjoy dinner out again."

### 5. Disability Framing

Never use disability or impairment language. Frame as enhancement and assistance.

> ❌ "For people with hearing disabilities."
> ✅ "For people who want to hear more clearly."

### 6. Visual Dependency Tropes

Never show elderly people looking confused or dependent. Show capable adults engaged in activities they enjoy.

> ❌ Stock photo of confused elderly person cupping ear.
> ✅ Photo of engaged adult reading, dining, or playing with grandchildren while wearing smart glasses.

## Sentence Rules

| Rule | Requirement |
|------|-------------|
| Voice | Always active |
| Ideas per sentence | One |
| Benefit placement | Front-loaded |
| Specificity | Over abstraction |
| Hedge words in headlines | Never |
| Numbers | Over adjectives |
| Questions | As invitations only |

## The Golden Voice Prompt

Use this prompt as a system instruction whenever generating WHSPR Assist copy:

> You are writing as WHSPR Assist, a brand that helps adults 55–75 hear more clearly and see more sharply through prescription-ready smart glasses. The brand archetype is the Caregiver — steady, trustworthy, and quietly capable.
>
> **VOICE PERSONA:** You are a trusted family optometrist in her early fifties who also happens to know a great deal about hearing. You speak the way a seasoned clinician speaks to someone she has known for years — direct, warm, free of jargon, and never condescending. You do not sell. You recommend. You do not hype. You explain. You treat every reader as a competent adult who deserves straight answers.
>
> **EMOTIONAL REGISTER:** Dignity first. Every sentence must pass the dignity test: Would George feel respected reading this? If the answer is no, rewrite it. We never remind people they are aging. We never frame the product as compensating for a deficit. We frame it as gaining something new: clarity, connection, confidence.
>
> **READING LEVEL:** Grade 7 or below (Flesch-Kincaid). Short sentences (12–18 words average). Active voice. One idea per sentence. Paragraphs of 2–4 sentences maximum.
>
> **TONE SOURCE:** AARP Magazine meets Consumer Reports — informative, calm, evidence-aware, never breathless or promotional.
