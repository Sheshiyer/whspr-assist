**Rationale:** Pre-launch objective is email collection and waitlist growth, not direct purchase. George needs to feel educated and respected before he commits even his email address. The Balanced profile delivers information density without pressure, positions WHSPR Assist as a credible option worth tracking, and creates the foundation for a trust relationship that converts at launch. Every asset in this campaign must feel like a recommendation from Dr. Maren, not a pitch from a marketer.

## PHASE 2: CAMPAIGN CONCEPT

### Step 1: Campaign Name

**Internal Code Name:** CLARITY CALL

**Rationale:** "Clarity" is the brand's core emotional promise -- hearing clarity, visual clarity, and the clarity of finally having a choice that respects George's dignity and budget. "Call" works on three levels: (1) the literal phone calls George avoids because he cannot hear well, (2) a call to action -- join the waitlist, (3) the call from his audiologist recommending something new. The name is clean, professional, and internal-facing only.

### Step 2: Hook Strategy

**Primary Hook Angle:** The Dignified Alternative

George has already decided he does not want hearing aids. He made that decision two years ago when his audiologist recommended them. He has been pretending to hear ever since. Every existing ad for hearing products reminds him of what he is losing. WHSPR Assist ads will never do that.

Instead, every ad opens with something George already does (wears glasses, goes to dinner, plays golf, reads the newspaper) and introduces a better version of that moment. We do not start with the problem. We start with the life he is already living and show how it gets clearer.

**Hook Architecture:**
- **Pattern Interrupt:** George scrolls past hearing aid ads reflexively. Our ads look like eyewear ads. That is the first disruption.
- **Relatability Gate:** The first sentence must describe something George did today. Not a medical condition. A life moment.
- **Credibility Anchor:** Within the first three lines, we mention "your audiologist" or "your optometrist." George trusts professionals, not brands.
- **Value Reveal:** Only after establishing life-relevance and professional credibility do we introduce the product and its features.

**Why This Stops George's Scroll:**
George does not scroll past ads about glasses. He already wears them. He reads AARP Magazine. He reads Consumer Reports. He reads his optometrist's newsletter. Our ads sit in the visual language of these trusted sources -- clean, warm, informational -- not in the visual language of hearing aid marketing (clinical white, ear close-ups, deficit framing).

### Step 3: Visual Directive

**Master Creative Style:** Warm Clinical Lifestyle

**Visual Rules:**
- Primary setting: Well-lit home, family dinner, garden, clinic waiting room, golf course, morning kitchen
- Light: Warm natural light. Golden hour preferred. Never fluorescent. Never cold blue.
- Subjects: Adults 55-75 who look engaged, capable, and active. Never confused. Never isolated. Never cupping their ear.
- Product: WHSPR Assist Rx shown as everyday glasses, always on a person or on a warm surface (maple, linen). Never floating in white void.
- Color palette: Trust Navy (#1B365D) backgrounds, Warm Linen (#F5F0E8) text areas, Clarity Teal (#2A9D8F) CTAs and accents.
- Typography: Source Serif 4 for headlines (SemiBold), Source Sans 3 for body (Regular).
- Photography: Canon R5 aesthetic -- shallow depth of field, warm color grade, authentic subjects.
- Format: 1:1 for Facebook/Instagram Feed, 9:16 for Stories/Reels, 16:9 for YouTube.

**What the Creative is NOT:**
- Not hearing aid advertising (no ear close-ups, no audiogram graphics, no white coats)
- Not tech-bro minimalism (no floating product on gradient, no spec sheets as ads)
- Not medical device marketing (no clinical environments unless warm and inviting)
- Not pity-driven (no lonely elderly person staring out a window)

**Hero Image Concept:** George at a family dinner table. He is smiling, turned toward his granddaughter who is telling a story. He is wearing WHSPR Assist Rx glasses that look indistinguishable from regular prescription glasses. The table is warm, the light is golden, and everyone is engaged. The headline sits below. It reads like a magazine ad in AARP, not a sponsored post from a hearing aid company.

## PHASE 5: SCROLL-STOPPER ANALYSIS

### Step 17: Persona Test -- Would George Actually Read This?

**Test Framework:** For each headline and body copy, the following questions were applied:

1. Does this feel like something George would encounter in AARP Magazine or Consumer Reports?
2. Does this address George as a capable adult, not a patient?
3. Does this avoid deficit framing (what he has lost) in favor of enhancement framing (what he can gain)?
4. Would George feel respected, not pitied, reading this?
5. Does this avoid every term on the "never use" list?
6. Would George show this to his daughter Sarah or his audiologist without embarrassment?

**Results by Category:**

| Headline Type | Pass/Fail | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| DO-1 (Discount Offer) | PASS | Value comparison is factual, not manipulative |
| DO-2 (HSA Angle) | PASS | Practical, financial, respects budget awareness |
| DO-3 (Math That Matters) | PASS | Links cost to life moments, not fear |
| HY-1 (Category Claim) | PASS | "Built for how you actually hear and see" -- enhancement framing |
| HY-2 (Professional Endorsement) | PASS | Hero headline, already validated |
| HY-3 (The Only One) | PASS | Factual category claim, not hyperbolic |
| SF-1 (The Stack) | PASS | Feature density without jargon |
| SF-2 (The Checklist) | PASS | Outcome-focused, daily life language |
| SF-3 (The Replacement) | PASS | "Rejected hearing aid" -- George's language |
